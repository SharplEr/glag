**G1** (Garbage-First) is the default garbage collector in modern HotSpot JVMs.
This text is written with **Java 11+** in mind and is closest in spirit to current
Oracle/OpenJDK documentation for **JDK 21-25**.

G1 tries to give a practical balance between **latency** and **throughput**.

G1 is a **generational**, **region-based**, and **mostly concurrent** garbage collector.
It splits the heap into many equal-sized regions. The exact region size is chosen ergonomically
from **1 MB to 32 MB**, with the JVM aiming for roughly **2048 regions** in total.

At a high level the heap is divided into:

1. **Eden** regions, where new objects are allocated.
2. **Survivor** regions, where some objects are copied after a young collection.
3. **Old** regions, where longer-lived objects end up.
4. **Humongous** regions, used for very large objects.
5. **Archive** regions, used for archived data.

**Eden** and **Survivor** belong to the **young generation**.
**Old** regions belong to the **old generation**.
Humongous objects are treated specially and are backed by humongous regions in the old generation area.

Most of the time G1 focuses on collecting the **young generation**.
When Eden fills up, G1 starts a stop-the-world evacuation pause and copies live objects out of the collected regions.
Some objects move to Survivor regions, and some may be promoted to Old regions.

You can see such a pause in the GC log:

```
Pause Young (Normal) (G1 Evacuation Pause) 6883M->752M(10240M) 4.196ms
```

After a young GC, Eden becomes available for new allocations again.

One of the main tuning ideas in G1 is that it adjusts the amount of work done during a pause
so that pauses stay closer to the target pause time.
In practice this often means changing the size of the young generation and the collection set.
If recent pauses are too long, G1 usually tries to do less work per pause.
If pauses are short enough, G1 can afford to do more work and reduce throughput overhead.

If your application has the usual generational behavior, where many objects die young,
G1 will often spend most of its time in young collections.

Eventually G1 also needs to reclaim space in the old generation.
For that it starts a **concurrent marking cycle**.
This phase runs mostly while the application is still working, but it still includes a few pauses.
The exact moment when marking starts depends on G1 heuristics and occupancy thresholds,
not simply on the old generation being completely full.

You may see lines like:

```
Concurrent Mark Cycle
Reclaimed 255 empty regions
Pause Remark 6932M->4892M(10240M) 64.999ms
Concurrent Mark Cycle 12840.693ms
```

So even a *concurrent* phase is not completely pause-free.

After marking, G1 may enter the **space-reclamation phase**, usually visible as **mixed collections**.
Mixed collections reclaim young regions and may also reclaim selected old regions with enough garbage.

For example:

```
Pause Young (Prepare Mixed) (G1 Evacuation Pause) 8423M->5628M(10240M) 29.131ms
Pause Young (Mixed) (G1 Evacuation Pause) 5900M->4699M(10240M) 25.553ms
```

One of the most dangerous situations is **To-space exhausted** or, more generally,
an evacuation failure.
This means G1 could not find enough destination space while trying to copy live objects out of the collection set.
That is usually a sign of heavy memory pressure, insufficient free space, or an undersized heap.
In the worst case this can lead to a **Full GC**, which is much more expensive than normal G1 pauses.

For example:

```
GC(101) To-space exhausted
GC(101) Pause Young (Normal) (GCLocker Initiated GC) 10187M->9983M(10240M) 62.297ms
GC(102) To-space exhausted
GC(102) Pause Young (Normal) (G1 Evacuation Pause) 10231M->10231M(10240M) 61.498ms
GC(103) Clear Next Bitmap 11.036ms
GC(103) Pause Full (G1 Compaction Pause) 10231M->1077M(10240M) 321.573ms
```
